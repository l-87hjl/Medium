The 13 Anomalies of 3I/ATLAS

(Organized Around Avi Loeb’s Framework, with Observations and Interpretations)

Harvard astrophysicist Avi Loeb has organized the unusual features of interstellar object 3I/ATLAS into a list of "anomalies" that he argues are collectively difficult to explain with familiar comet physics. The following list synthesizes his November 2025 analysis, including his estimated probabilities ($P$) for each event occurring naturally.

1. Retrograde Ecliptic Alignment

Likelihood: 0.2% (P = 0.002)

Loeb’s explanation

“Its retrograde trajectory is aligned to within 5 degrees with the ecliptic plane of the planets around the Sun, with a likelihood of 0.2%.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: 3I/ATLAS orbits the Sun in the opposite direction to the planets (retrograde) but lies almost perfectly flat within their orbital plane (the ecliptic).
  • Instruments: Orbital solutions from the ATLAS network and Minor Planet Center data.
  • Context: Most retrograde objects (like comets from the Oort cloud) enter the solar system at random, steep angles, not flat within the planetary disk.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb argues this geometric alignment is statistically rare for a random capture.
  • Mainstream astronomers caution that observational biases might favor the discovery of objects in the ecliptic, though the retrograde nature makes this specific combination unusual.

2. Persistent Sunward Jet (Anti-tail)

Likelihood: <0.1% (P < 0.001)

Loeb’s explanation

“During July and August as well as through November of 2025, 3I/ATLAS displayed a sunward jet (anti-tail) that is not an optical illusion from geometric perspective, unlike thousands of known comets.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: A distinct stream of material was seen pointing toward the Sun, rather than away from it like a typical comet tail. This persisted even as the viewing angle changed.
  • Instruments: Deep imaging from amateur and professional observatories (e.g., Celestron EdgeHD 800, large survey telescopes).

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb speculates this could be a propulsive beam or a protective shield against solar radiation.
  • Natural explanations involve large dust grains that aren't pushed back by sunlight as easily, or "anisotropic sublimation" (ice vaporizing from only one specific spot).

3. Anomalous Mass Relative to Previous Interstellar Objects

Likelihood: <0.1% (P < 0.001)

Loeb’s explanation

“The nucleus of 3I/ATLAS is roughly a million times more massive than 1I/ 'Oumuamua and a thousand times more massive than 2I/Borisov, while moving faster than both... There is not enough rocky material in interstellar space to deliver a rock of this mass once per decade to the inner solar system.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The object is significantly heavier (more massive) than the previous two visitors, implying a very large or dense object.
  • Instruments: Photometric modeling and orbital dynamics analysis.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb argues that 3I/ATLAS is too big and too fast to be a random "draw" from the expected population of interstellar debris.
  • Critics suggest our sample size (3 objects) is too small to define what is "normal" yet.

4. Fine-Tuned Planetary Encounter Geometry

Likelihood: 0.005% (P = 0.00005)

Loeb’s explanation

“The arrival time of 3I/ATLAS was fine-tuned to bring it within tens of millions of kilometers from Mars, Venus and Jupiter and be unobservable from Earth at perihelion.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The trajectory threaded the needle past multiple planets at close range and hid behind the Sun during its closest approach.
  • Instruments: Ephemeris data from JPL Horizons; observations from Mars orbiters (MRO, TGO).

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb suggests this maximizes the object's ability to "scan" multiple worlds while minimizing scrutiny from Earth.
  • The mainstream view treats this as a coincidental result of the Sun's gravity bending the object's path.

5. Unusual Nickel-to-Iron and Nickel-to-Cyanide Ratios

Likelihood: <0.1% (P < 0.001)

Loeb’s explanation

“The gas plume surrounding 3I/ATLAS contains much more nickel than iron as found in industrially-produced nickel alloys, and a nickel to cyanide ratio that is orders of magnitude larger than for thousands of known comets.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: Spectral lines show an abundance of nickel but very little iron, a composition unlike natural solar system comets.
  • Instruments: Spectroscopy from large observatories (e.g., VLT).

Explanations and interpretations

  • This composition resembles artificial alloys (like stainless steel) more than typical cometary dust (which is usually iron-rich).

6. Extremely Low Water Content

Likelihood: Unquantified in recent list (Grouped with Composition)

Loeb’s explanation

“Its gas plume contains only 4% water by mass, a primary constituent of familiar comets.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: Despite being active, the object releases very little water vapor compared to standard comets, which are mostly water ice.
  • Instruments: Infrared (JWST) and radio (MeerKAT) spectroscopy.

Explanations and interpretations

  • This supports the "unusual composition" argument. Natural explanations suggest it might be covered in a "crust" processed by cosmic rays, or formed in a region dominated by CO2 rather than water.

7. Extreme Negative Polarization

Likelihood: <1% (P < 0.01)

Loeb’s explanation

“It shows extreme negative polarization, unprecedented for all known comets, including 2I/Borisov.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: Light reflected from the object is polarized in the "negative" direction to a degree (-2.7%) much deeper than normal comets.
  • Instruments: Polarimetry from telescopes like the VLT (FORS2).

Explanations and interpretations

  • Polarization reveals surface texture; this reading suggests unique surface properties, possibly very small, dark, or transparent grains.

8. Directional Coincidence with the “Wow!” Signal

Likelihood: 0.6% (P = 0.006)

Loeb’s explanation

“It arrived from a direction coincident with the radio 'Wow! Signal' to within 9 degrees.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The object came from the constellation Sagittarius, roughly the same patch of sky where the unexplained 1977 radio signal originated.
  • Instruments: Historical radio data vs. orbital backtracking.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb notes this as a "geometric coincidence" that might link the object to a sender.

9. Unprecedented Brightening Rate and Blue Color

Likelihood: <10% (P < 0.1)

Loeb’s explanation

“Near perihelion, 3I/ATLAS brightened faster than any known comet and was bluer than the Sun... This might be a signature that its engine turned on.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: As it got close to the Sun, it got bright much faster than expected and turned a distinct blue color.
  • Instruments: Photometry (light curves) and color filters.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Natural comets usually turn redder (due to dust). A blue color suggests gas emission, but the intensity is extreme. Loeb suggests this could be engine exhaust.

10. Jet Mass Flux Requiring Unreasonably Large Surface Area

Likelihood: <10% (P < 0.1)

Loeb’s explanation

“3I/ATLAS exhibits sunward and anti-solar jets which require an unreasonably large surface area for a natural comet in order to absorb enough sunlight needed to sublimate enough ice to feed the mass flux of these jets.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The amount of stuff shooting off the comet is too high to be powered just by the sunlight hitting it.
  • Instruments: Deep imaging of the tail/coma structure.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb implies this "energy budget crisis" points toward internal power sources rather than passive solar heating.

11. Non-Gravitational Acceleration Without Corresponding Mass Loss

Likelihood: <10% (P < 0.1)

Loeb’s explanation

“Near perihelion 3I/ATLAS exhibits non-gravitational acceleration which requires massive evaporation... whereas preliminary images indicate that the object maintained its integrity and did not break up.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The object sped up (or pushed itself) more than gravity explains. Normally, a comet pushing this hard would lose so much mass (~13%) it would fall apart, but 3I/ATLAS stayed intact.
  • Instruments: Astrometry (tracking position over time).

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb argues this points to an efficient propulsion system that doesn't involve shedding the whole outer layer of the object.

12. Jet Orientation Not Smeared by Rotation

Likelihood: <10% (P < 0.1)

Loeb’s explanation

“The tightly-collimated jets from 3I/ATLAS maintain orientation across a million kilometers in multiple directions relative to the Sun despite its measured rotation.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The comet spins every ~16 hours, but the jets stay straight and focused.
  • Instruments: Time-lapse imaging.

Explanations and interpretations

  • On a spinning snowball, jets should spiral like a garden sprinkler. Steady jets suggest active steering or nozzles that pivot to stay locked on a target direction.

13. Coincidence with Jupiter's Hill Radius (The "Extraordinary" Anomaly)

Likelihood: 0.004% (P = 0.00004)

Loeb’s explanation

“The forecasted perijove distance of 3I/ATLAS during its encounter with Jupiter on March 16, 2026 is 53.445 (+/-0.06) million kilometers, identical to Jupiter's Hill radius, 53.502 million kilometers... This match was enabled by the non-gravitational acceleration that 3I/ATLAS displayed near perihelion.”

Plain-language observations & instruments

  • Observation: The object's path takes it to the exact edge of Jupiter's gravitational dominance (the Hill sphere). This is the specific boundary where a spacecraft would need to be to enter a stable orbit or release probes with minimal energy.
  • Instruments: Precise trajectory forecasting using NASA/JPL Horizons data.

Explanations and interpretations

  • Loeb interprets this as a potential "mothership" maneuver. By hitting this exact distance, the object is perfectly positioned to seed Jupiter's system with small probes or satellites.
  • This is currently the most statistically significant anomaly in Loeb's analysis.
