What This Page Is (and Is Not)

This is NOT a JPL Small-Body Database solution.

This is a reconstruction of the earliest published orbital state for C/2025 N1 (ATLAS), based on:

  • MPEC 2025-N12 (Minor Planet Electronic Circular, issued July 2, 2025)
  • Early observation arc: 122 observations spanning June 14 – July 2, 2025
  • Purely gravitational orbit — no nongravitational force modeling
  • Published by the Minor Planet Center (MPC), not JPL

Purpose: This solution represents the moment of interstellar confirmation and serves as the historical anchor for all subsequent JPL solutions (40+). It demonstrates how quickly the object's orbital parameters stabilized and shows the epistemic transition from discovery to refined modeling.

Methodological Note: Solution 1 represents a different class of orbital determination than JPL Solutions 40-44.
  • MPC early-arc orbit vs. JPL refined solutions
  • No nongravitational forces modeled (A1, A2, A3 do not exist at this stage)
  • Shorter observation arc (18 days vs. 200+ days)
  • Larger uncertainties expected and normal for discovery-stage orbits
  • Classification (hyperbolic/interstellar) confirmed, not overturned by later solutions

Nongravitational Model Parameters

NOT APPLICABLE AT THIS SOLUTION STAGE

MPEC 2025-N12 uses a purely gravitational orbit model. Nongravitational parameters (A1, A2, A3) do not exist at this stage of orbital determination.

Why this is normal: Early-arc discovery orbits typically assume gravity-only motion until:

  • Sufficient observation arc length (months, not days)
  • Observable deviations from pure gravity
  • Physical evidence of outgassing or volatile activity

JPL Solutions 40+ introduce CO₂ sublimation modeling with A1, A2, A3 parameters based on 200+ day arcs.

Physical Parameters

NOT PUBLISHED IN MPEC 2025-N12

Photometric parameters (M1 comet total magnitude, K1 magnitude slope) are not included in this early MPC orbital circular.

These parameters require longer-term photometric monitoring and appear in later JPL SBDB solutions.

Known Limitations (Expected for Discovery Orbits)

This solution is expected to be refined by later observations — but the core classification remains valid.

  • Short observation arc (18 days vs. 200+ days in later solutions)
  • Large uncertainty envelope (uncertainties not published in MPEC)
  • No nongravitational modeling (assumed pure gravity)
  • Subject to rapid revision as more observations accumulate
  • Eccentricity appears higher (6.28 vs. ~6.14 in later solutions) due to missing NG forces

What did NOT change: The hyperbolic/interstellar classification was confirmed, not overturned, by all subsequent solutions.

Why Solution 1 Still Matters

Historical and Scientific Significance:

  • Moment of interstellar confirmation — first published orbit confirming hyperbolic trajectory
  • Anchors the solution sequence — without Solution 1, Solutions 40+ appear ex nihilo
  • Demonstrates epistemic continuity — later solutions refine but don't overturn
