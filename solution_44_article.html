December 19: What It Is and What It Is Not

"December 19 does not decide whether we should ask different questions. It decides whether we are allowed to avoid them."
üé≠ December 19 is Schr√∂dinger's validation: simultaneously decisive and completely insufficient, depending on who's talking and what they want to be true.

What December 19 Is

December 19 is a test of short-term astrometric prediction. That's it.

It tests whether the CO‚ÇÇ nongravitational model can reproduce 3I/ATLAS's sky position within observational uncertainties. If the object appears where JPL predicts it will appear, within the predicted margins, then the model works for predicting positions.

This is a legitimate reason for naturalists to say: "The model works."

And it would be true. The model does work, at least for this purpose.

What December 19 Is Not

December 19 is not a test of origin. It is not a test of intent. It is not a test of long-term behavior. It is not a resolution of physical mechanism ambiguity.

üé≠ December 19 tests one thing: can a CO‚ÇÇ model predict where a dot appears on the sky? Confusing those categories is like thinking a GPS location proves someone is happy. The data tells where; everything else is interpretation.
"Passing the test validates a fit, not an explanation."
üé≠ If the model passes, half the internet will declare victory for naturalism. If it fails, the other half will declare victory for curiosity. Both will be wrong, but at least they'll be consistent.

Here's what December 19 actually validates if it goes well: JPL found a mathematical function that reproduces the observed trajectory. Full stop. That mathematical function happens to be called "CO‚ÇÇ sublimation," but the test itself doesn't distinguish between "CO‚ÇÇ actually sublimating" and "some other force that happens to produce the same acceleration profile."

This is not pedantry. This is the difference between operational success and ontological truth.

Avoiding Goalpost Shifting: The Fairness Section

Two conditions must align on December 19 to support the naturalist narrative:

1. Mathematical Alignment

Observed sky position matches JPL predictions within reasonable margins.

This is the minimum bar. If 3I/ATLAS appears significantly off from where Solution 44 predicts, then the model has failed at its primary job: predicting positions. That would be bad for the CO‚ÇÇ hypothesis.

2. Physical Consistency

Observations do not contradict the implied outgassing (or its proxies). No glaring mismatch between required thrust and observed physical signatures.

This is the harder bar. The nongravitational acceleration implies substantial mass loss. Solution 44 requires sustained outgassing at a level that should produce detectable signatures: a visible coma, spectroscopic evidence of gas, brightness variations consistent with active venting.

If the astrometry matches but the physical observations show nothing compatible with the required mass loss, then we have a problem. The model predicts positions correctly while failing to predict everything else.

If Both Align

It is reasonable to say: "Within the model's rationality, this looks natural."

üé≠ The phrase "within the model's rationality" is doing a truly heroic amount of work in that sentence. It's the diplomatic equivalent of saying "technically correct, which is the most exhausting kind of correct."

But seriously: if both conditions align, this is not institutional failure. This is not moving goalposts. It's legitimate model validation within the scope of what the model claims to predict.

Closing: Where the Exam Ends and the Syllabus Becomes the Problem

Solution 44 is where the exam ends and the syllabus becomes the problem.

JPL can calculate trajectories to nine decimal places. They can fit models that reproduce astrometric positions with residuals of 0.4 arcseconds. They can predict where an interstellar object will appear in the sky months in advance.

The question is whether we're willing to ask what those decimals mean.

Solution 44 confirms that Solution 42 was already converged. The model is stable. The parameters are tightening. The December 19 test approaches with a clean baseline.

But here's what hasn't changed:

  • Three-axis nongravitational acceleration (A1, A2, A3 all nonzero)
  • Anomalously high A2/A1 ratio (~0.41)
  • Persistent out-of-plane thrust
  • Weak or absent classical coma relative to required mass loss
  • Smooth, low-noise acceleration profile
  • Jupiter Hill-sphere coincidence (one-in-25,000 geometry)
  • Second interstellar object exhibiting nongravitational acceleration without textbook signatures

None of these disappear because JPL added five more observations. These are global features, not test-epoch features. They're structural properties of the object's behavior, and they remain whether December 19 goes well or poorly.

If December 19 validates the model (and there's a decent chance it will), the natural response from most astronomers will be: "See? It works. Case closed."

But fitting the data is not the same as explaining the object. A model can be operationally successful without being ontologically complete. The CO‚ÇÇ hypothesis may predict positions perfectly while leaving deeper questions unanswered: Why this geometry? Why this coincidence? Why this ratio? Why this object, and why now?

Science has a habit of declaring victory at the operational level and calling it epistemological closure. The model works, therefore the model is true, therefore no further inquiry is warranted. This works fine for most objects. But 3I/ATLAS isn't most objects.

Both required increasingly exotic natural explanations.

One anomalous interstellar object is an outlier. Two is a pattern.

The exam is almost over. JPL has done their homework. December 19 will show whether the model survives contact with nature.

But passing the exam doesn't fix the syllabus. And the syllabus is starting to look like it was written by someone who never expected interstellar objects to show up asking hard questions.
