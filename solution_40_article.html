What Solution 40 Actually Says

JPL's latest solution incorporates 751 observations through December 6. That's a staggering amount of data. That's "we really, really measured this thing" levels of precision. The residuals come in at 0.40998, which in orbit-fitting terms translates to "yeah, we nailed it."

But Solution 40 does more than just fit positions. It commits to a specific physical model:

Three-axis non-gravitational acceleration: A1 = +4.64×10⁻⁸ au/day² (radial), A2 = +1.93×10⁻⁸ au/day² (transverse), A3 = -5.06×10⁻⁹ au/day² (out-of-plane).

CO₂ sublimation law: g(r) = (1/r)², the standard form for carbon dioxide outgassing.

Required mass loss: ~150 kg/s total gas production (CO₂: 129 kg/s, CO: 14 kg/s, H₂O: 6.6 kg/s, based on JWST observations).

This isn't hedged language or "unknown volatile" hand-waving. JPL picked CO₂ specifically, fit three dimensions of acceleration, and published exact numbers with uncertainties (https://ssd.jpl.nasa.gov/tools/sbdb_lookup.html#/?sstr=3i&view=OPC). Producer: Davide Farnocchia, one of the world's top experts in orbit determination.

They showed their work. All of it.

Why CO₂ Makes Sense

The CO₂ model isn't "unobtainium from planet Handwavius." It's literally the stuff in your soda. At 3I's current distance (perihelion at 1.36 AU, now ~1.8 AU), water ice would be barely active. CO₂ is the volatile that keeps going like the Energizer Bunny, exactly what you'd need for sustained acceleration this far from the Sun.

The g(r) = (1/r)² law isn't a fudge factor either. It's actual sublimation physics, the right functional form for CO₂ at these distances. JPL did the homework correctly.

Most importantly, the mass budget actually works. At ~150 kg/s loss over months, a 0.5-1 km nucleus loses only ~0.6% of its mass. Unlike ʻOumuamua's "hydrogen iceberg" or "nitrogen ice" proposals (which Loeb demolished by showing they'd sublimate away in less than 20 million years), 3I's mass budget is internally consistent.

The physics works. That's JPL's strength.

Where the Tension Lives

But then there are the oddities. Each individually explainable, but together forming a pattern that makes you squint.

The missing coma: CO₂ sublimates, produces gas, gas should scatter light, multiple observatories (including HST) should detect something. Result? Nothing. It's like claiming someone's cooking a steak but there's no smoke, no smell, no sizzle. Could be sous vide. Could also be... not a steak.

The A2/A1 ratio: Normal comets show transverse-to-radial acceleration ratios around 0.03-0.05. Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko? 0.035. 3I/ATLAS? 0.42. That's not slightly high, that's "please explain yourself" high. Roughly 10 times what you'd expect. To get a ratio that high from jets, you need geometry so specific it's like throwing darts blindfolded and hitting bullseye. Possible? Sure. Convenient? Hmm.

The magnitude behavior: The brightness variation (K1=4.5) suggests significant activity, but the coma detector says 404 not found.

The pattern: One weird thing equals quirky. Two weird things equals interesting. Three-plus weird things equals "are we being gaslit by a space rock?"

JPL isn't dumb. They see the same tensions we do. They're just doing the math and going "well, THIS is what fits..." And they put it on the record: Solution 40, all public, all traceable, producer credited.

The Jupiter Coincidence (Or: Why March 16 Also Matters)

Solution 40's parameters don't just predict December 19. They also determine where 3I will be on March 16, 2026, when it reaches Jupiter. And here's where it gets genuinely weird.

With the NG acceleration included, 3I approaches Jupiter at 53.445 (±0.06) million km. Jupiter's Hill radius on that date? 53.502 million km.

These match within one standard deviation.

The Hill radius is the boundary where Jupiter's gravity dominates over the Sun's. Inside that radius, Jupiter can capture satellites or debris. Outside, the Sun pulls them away. It's the edge of Jupiter's gravitational domain.

Without the NG acceleration, 3I would have missed this radius entirely. The acceleration shifted the trajectory by roughly 0.1 million km. Just enough to bring 3I to the edge of Jupiter's Hill sphere.

Statistical likelihood of this coincidence: less than 0.00004 (0.004%). One in 25,000.

So the NG acceleration not only explains 3I's motion, it also "steered" it to Jupiter's Hill sphere. Neat trick for sublimating ice.

Statisticians will say "0.004% isn't impossible!" And they're right. But it's WEIRD though. It's like throwing a dart at a dartboard from across the room, blindfolded, while spinning, and hitting bullseye. Could happen! Probably won't.

If you wanted to visit Jupiter's neighborhood without actually hitting Jupiter, this is exactly where you'd aim.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: "The comet is headed exactly where natural physics says it should go" and "the comet is headed to a one-in-25,000 coincidence at Jupiter's gravity boundary" are both technically true statements. Both accurate. Both deeply uncomfortable.

Why this matters: If Solution 40's CO₂ model holds through December, then March 16 becomes the next test. Does 3I arrive at Jupiter where Solution 40 predicts? Does the NG acceleration continue following the (1/r)² law? Or does something change?
